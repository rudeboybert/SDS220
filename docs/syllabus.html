Course Description & Objectives

  • Official course description: On Smith College Course Search.
  • Objectives (slides): This semester you will
    1. Learn statistical inference through data science, not mathematics/probability theory.
    2. Engage in the data/science research pipeline in as faithful a manner as possible while maintaining a level suitable for novices.
    3. Develop your statistical literacy, a necessary ability for effective citizenship.

Materials

We will draw from the following sources:

  1. “ModernDive: An Introduction to Statistical and Data Sciences via R” by Ismay and Kim (click link in menubar above).
  2. “OpenIntro: Introductory Statistics with Randomization and Simulation” by Diez, Barr, and Cetinkaya-Rundel
  3. DataCamp: an online interactive environment for learning data science currently via R and Python. On top of the DataCamp courses we’ll cover this semester, you have free access to all their courses for 6 months.

Expectations

As a student in this class:

  1. Communication: You are expected to stay on top of announcements and direct messages in Slack.
  2. Attendance: You are not required to inform the instructor of occasional absenses. However, you are resposible for asking your peers, and not the instructor, for what you missed.
  3. Collaboration: While I encourage you to work with your peers for problem sets and labs, you must submit your own answers and not simple rewordings of another’s work. Furthermore, all collaborations must be explicitly acknowledged in your submissions.
  4. Honor Code: All your work must follow the Smith College Academic Honor Code Statement; in particular all external sources must be cited in your submissions.

Engagement 5%

It is difficult to explicit codify what constitutes “an engaged student,” so instead I present the following rough principle I will follow: you’ll only get out of this class as much as you put in. Some examples of behavior counter to this principle:

  • Not participating in class.
  • Engaging so little with me, either in class or during office hours, that I don’t know what your voice sounds like.
  • Shirking group work.
  • Ghosting.
  • Doing the bare minimum to get by.
